What BMI Qualifies for Zepbound?

The short answer: Zepbound℠ (tirzepatide injection) is FDA-approved for chronic weight management in adults with a BMI of 30 or higher (obesity), or a BMI of 27 or higher with at least one weight-related comorbidity such as hypertension, type 2 diabetes, dyslipidemia, cardiovascular disease, or obstructive sleep apnea. It was also approved in June 2024 as the first-ever drug treatment for moderate-to-severe obstructive sleep apnea in adults with obesity — a landmark expansion of its indications. Zepbound contains the same active ingredient as Mounjaro℠ (tirzepatide) and has produced the highest average weight-loss outcomes of any FDA-approved weight-management medication in clinical history.

FDA-Approved Indications and BMI Thresholds for Zepbound

Zepbound℠ (tirzepatide injection, 2.5 mg to 15 mg once weekly) was first approved by the FDA in November 2023 for chronic weight management in adults. As of May 2026, Zepbound has two distinct FDA-approved indications:

  1. Chronic weight management — in adults with obesity (BMI ≥ 30 kg/m²), or overweight (BMI ≥ 27 kg/m²) with at least one weight-related comorbidity, as an adjunct to a reduced-calorie diet and increased physical activity
  2. Moderate-to-severe obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) — in adults with obesity (BMI ≥ 30 kg/m²), approved in June 2024, making Zepbound the first drug ever approved to treat OSA

The OSA indication is clinically significant not only for patients with sleep apnea, but also from a coverage perspective: patients who cannot obtain Zepbound under their plan's weight-management drug benefit may have an alternative pathway through the OSA indication, depending on their insurer.

Zepbound's Second Indication: Obstructive Sleep Apnea

The June 2024 FDA approval of Zepbound for moderate-to-severe OSA in adults with obesity (BMI ≥ 30) was historic — no drug had ever before been approved to treat sleep apnea. The approval was based on the SURMOUNT-OSA trials, which demonstrated significant reductions in the apnea-hypopnea index (AHI — the number of breathing interruptions per hour of sleep) in patients treated with tirzepatide. Patients using CPAP therapy as well as those not using CPAP both showed meaningful AHI reductions. For the approximately 38 million Americans living with sleep apnea, this creates a new treatment option and, critically, a new insurance coverage pathway for tirzepatide.

Qualifying weight-related comorbidities for the BMI 27–29.9 threshold (weight management indication) include: hypertension, type 2 diabetes mellitus, dyslipidemia, established cardiovascular disease, and obstructive sleep apnea. These are explicitly listed in the Eli Lilly FDA approval announcement and the Zepbound prescribing information.

How BMI Is Calculated and What Your Number Means

Body Mass Index is the primary gating criterion for Zepbound eligibility under both FDA-approved indications. Unlike Mounjaro or Ozempic — which are diagnosis-driven — Zepbound's eligibility is explicitly BMI-driven, similar to Wegovy.

Metric: BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height (m)²

Imperial: BMI = (weight in lbs × 703) ÷ height in inches²

A practical example: a person who is 5 feet 6 inches tall and weighs 200 lbs has a BMI of approximately 32.3 — Class I obesity, clearly within Zepbound's BMI ≥ 30 threshold. At 175 lbs, that same person would have a BMI of 28.3 — in the overweight range, requiring a documented comorbidity (e.g., a hypertension diagnosis) to meet Zepbound's eligibility criteria.

While BMI is the dominant criteria for Zepbound eligibility and prior authorization, it is worth noting that the American Medical Association has stated that BMI alone should not be used as a clinical measure and that additional measures of metabolic health — waist circumference, body fat percentage, visceral fat imaging — should supplement BMI when possible. However, for insurance prior authorization purposes, a documented BMI from a clinical visit remains the primary requirement. To check your current BMI, use our free BMI calculator.

What If Your BMI Is Below the Threshold?

If your BMI is below 27 and you do not have type 2 diabetes, Zepbound is not FDA-approved for your use. However, several considerations apply:

Comorbidities That Qualify at BMI 27–29.9

At BMI 27–29.9 (the overweight range), any of the following documented comorbidities makes you eligible for Zepbound under the FDA weight-management label:

  • Hypertension — Stage 1 or Stage 2 hypertension documented in your medical record
  • Type 2 diabetes mellitus — also opens access to Mounjaro (same tirzepatide molecule)
  • Dyslipidemia — elevated LDL, elevated triglycerides, or low HDL
  • Established cardiovascular disease — prior myocardial infarction, stroke, peripheral arterial disease, or coronary artery disease diagnosis
  • Obstructive sleep apnea — OSA documented by a sleep study; note that the OSA-specific indication requires BMI ≥ 30, but OSA as a comorbidity qualifies you for the weight-management indication at BMI 27+

The NHLBI recognizes that even overweight patients (BMI 27–29.9) with metabolic comorbidities carry meaningful cardiovascular risk that pharmacological intervention can address. If you are at BMI 27–29.9 with one or more of these conditions, your likelihood of insurance approval for Zepbound is substantially higher than for patients without documented comorbidities.

Step Therapy Requirements

Prior authorization for Zepbound almost universally requires documentation of step therapy — evidence that lifestyle-based interventions were attempted prior to pharmacological treatment. Most plans accept 6 months of structured dietary counseling or participation in a qualified weight-management program. Some plans additionally require prior failure of lower-cost medications such as phentermine-topiramate or bupropion-naltrexone. Your provider's prior authorization team can submit a letter of medical necessity that explains the clinical rationale, including your comorbidities, functional limitations, and prior treatment history.

What If Your BMI Is Above 40?

Class III obesity (BMI ≥ 40) represents one of the highest-burden states of excess adiposity, and Zepbound is particularly relevant at this BMI range. Patients with BMI ≥ 40 meet both the weight-management and (if they have sleep apnea) the OSA indications for Zepbound. Clinical trial data show that patients with higher baseline BMIs often achieve greater absolute weight loss in pounds, even though the percentage loss is similar across BMI categories.

At BMI ≥ 40, bariatric surgical evaluation is strongly recommended alongside consideration of pharmacotherapy. The American Society for Metabolic and Bariatric Surgery (ASMBS) has noted that GLP-1 and dual GIP/GLP-1 medications like tirzepatide represent a meaningful new option for patients who are not surgical candidates or who prefer to avoid surgery, but that surgical options remain available and appropriate for patients with the highest disease burden. Some patients choose sequential therapy — GLP-1 medication first to reduce perioperative risk, followed by bariatric surgery for durable long-term outcomes.

Insurance Coverage for Zepbound by BMI Bracket

Zepbound has one of the more complex insurance landscapes of any recently approved medication, given that it has two distinct FDA indications and involves rapidly evolving payer policies.

Commercial Insurance — Weight Management Indication

As of mid-2026, most major commercial carriers (UnitedHealth, Aetna, Cigna, BCBS, Humana) cover Zepbound for weight management with prior authorization. Required documentation includes: BMI from a clinical encounter, ICD-10 codes (E66.x for obesity and comorbidity codes), evidence of prior diet and exercise intervention, and — at some plans — evidence of prior pharmacotherapy failure. Prior authorization is typically processed within 1–3 weeks. Plan formularies vary, and some employers exclude GLP-1 weight-management drugs from their benefit design even when the insurer generally covers them.

Commercial Insurance — Sleep Apnea Indication

The OSA indication (BMI ≥ 30 with moderate-to-severe obstructive sleep apnea) creates a second coverage pathway. Some commercial plans that exclude weight-loss drugs may cover Zepbound under their sleep medicine or respiratory drug benefits. This is an evolving area; consult your benefits team or a prior authorization specialist to determine whether this pathway is available for your plan.

Medicare Part D

Medicare Part D does not cover weight-loss drugs under current law. However, Zepbound's OSA indication creates a potential coverage argument for Medicare beneficiaries with obesity and documented sleep apnea — depending on how CMS classifies the coverage request. This remains a developing area of policy. Proposed legislation (the TREAT Act and the Obesity Care for All Act) could expand Medicare weight-management drug coverage if enacted.

Medicaid

State Medicaid programs show variable coverage. A growing number of states have added tirzepatide to their obesity drug formularies in 2025–2026, though prior authorization requirements remain standard. The OSA indication may provide an alternative pathway in states whose Medicaid programs cover OSA medications but not obesity medications.

For a complete, up-to-date guide to Zepbound insurance coverage including prior authorization templates and state-by-state Medicaid maps, see insuranceglp1.com/zepbound-coverage.html.

Real-World Expectations: How Much Weight Loss to Expect on Zepbound

Zepbound's weight-loss data from the SURMOUNT clinical trial program represent the most impressive outcomes ever recorded for a pharmacological treatment of obesity. The trials consistently demonstrated outcomes previously associated only with bariatric surgery.

In SURMOUNT-1 — the landmark registration trial that supported Zepbound's approval — 2,539 adults with obesity or overweight with comorbidities (without T2D) were randomized to once-weekly tirzepatide at doses of 5 mg, 10 mg, or 15 mg, or placebo. Results at 72 weeks, published in and summarized by the American College of Cardiology's clinical trial summary:

  • 5 mg tirzepatide: average 15% body weight reduction; 85.1% achieved ≥5% weight loss
  • 10 mg tirzepatide: average 19.5% body weight reduction; 88.9% achieved ≥5% weight loss
  • 15 mg tirzepatide: average 20.9% body weight reduction; 90.9% achieved ≥5% weight loss; 56.7% achieved ≥20% weight loss
  • Placebo: average 3.1% body weight reduction; 34.5% achieved ≥5% weight loss

SURMOUNT-2 evaluated tirzepatide in adults with obesity and type 2 diabetes, showing 13.4% average weight loss at the highest dose — lower than the non-diabetic cohort but still substantially outperforming semaglutide in T2D populations studied in comparable timeframes.

A landmark 2024 real-world comparative study in JAMA Internal Medicine analyzed 18,386 tirzepatide users and 24,904 semaglutide users with overweight or obesity. Tirzepatide users were 63% more likely to achieve 5% or greater weight loss within one year (71.1% vs. 56.4%), confirming the SURMOUNT superiority signal in a real-world, diverse population.

Maintenance After Reaching Goal Weight

The SURMOUNT clinical trial program includes a specific maintenance sub-study — SURMOUNT-4 — that directly evaluated what happens when Zepbound is stopped after initial treatment. Participants who had lost weight during an initial tirzepatide treatment period were randomized to either continue tirzepatide or switch to placebo (simulating discontinuation).

The results, registered at ClinicalTrials.gov as NCT04784047, showed that participants who switched to placebo regained approximately 14 percentage points of body weight over the following 52 weeks, while those who continued tirzepatide maintained their weight loss and achieved an additional 5.5% reduction. This data clearly demonstrates that stopping tirzepatide after reaching goal weight is associated with substantial weight regain — consistent with the similar findings in the STEP semaglutide extension study.

Importantly, the research from University of Cambridge (eClinicalMedicine, 2026) modeling all GLP-1 class drugs showed rapid initial weight regain after stopping, with a plateau at approximately 75% of original weight loss by 60–80 weeks. Roughly 25% of the initial weight loss may be sustained permanently even without continuing medication. This means patients who stop Zepbound are not starting from zero — but they will likely need a robust behavioral maintenance program to preserve as much of their progress as possible.

Best practices for weight maintenance after Zepbound — supported by the Obesity Medicine Association's clinical practice guidelines — include protein-forward dietary patterns (targeting 1.2–1.6 g/kg body weight), resistance training at least 3 times per week to preserve lean mass, regular monitoring of metabolic markers, and scheduled follow-up appointments with your prescribing provider. Some patients successfully transition to lower doses of tirzepatide for long-term maintenance rather than stopping entirely.

How to Find a Zepbound Prescriber

Zepbound is a prescription medication available through licensed healthcare providers including physicians (MD/DO), nurse practitioners, and physician assistants. Primary care providers, obesity medicine specialists, and endocrinologists are the most common prescribers. Telehealth providers have dramatically expanded access for patients in areas with limited obesity medicine capacity.

Frequently Asked Questions: Zepbound BMI Eligibility

What BMI do you need for Zepbound?

Zepbound's FDA-approved BMI thresholds are: BMI ≥ 30 for the weight management and sleep apnea indications (no comorbidity required), or BMI ≥ 27 with at least one weight-related comorbidity for the weight management indication. Qualifying comorbidities include hypertension, type 2 diabetes, dyslipidemia, established cardiovascular disease, and obstructive sleep apnea. These thresholds are explicitly stated in the FDA approval documentation from Eli Lilly.

How much weight can you lose on Zepbound?

SURMOUNT-1 showed average body weight loss of 20.9% at the highest dose (15 mg tirzepatide) over 72 weeks — approximately 48 lbs on a mean starting weight of 231 lbs. At the lowest studied dose (5 mg), average weight loss was 15%. Over half of participants on the highest dose achieved ≥20% body weight reduction. At 10 mg, average loss was 19.5%. Real-world outcomes from a 2024 JAMA Internal Medicine study were consistent with clinical trial data, with 71.1% of tirzepatide users achieving ≥5% weight loss within one year.

Can Zepbound treat sleep apnea?

Yes. The FDA approved Zepbound in June 2024 as the first-ever drug treatment for moderate-to-severe obstructive sleep apnea in adults with obesity (BMI ≥ 30). The SURMOUNT-OSA trials showed significant reductions in the apnea-hypopnea index (AHI) in patients using tirzepatide compared to placebo. This means Zepbound can be prescribed for OSA — which may create a coverage pathway for patients who cannot obtain it under their plan's weight-management drug benefit.

What is the difference between Zepbound and Mounjaro?

Zepbound and Mounjaro contain the identical active ingredient: tirzepatide, at the same doses (2.5 mg through 15 mg weekly). The difference is solely the FDA-approved indication: Mounjaro for type 2 diabetes, Zepbound for weight management and obstructive sleep apnea. If you have type 2 diabetes and want tirzepatide, Mounjaro is your on-label choice with the best coverage. If you want tirzepatide for weight management without T2D, Zepbound has the explicit approval. See our Mounjaro BMI guide for a complete comparison.

Is Zepbound better than Wegovy?

Clinical data favor Zepbound (tirzepatide) over Wegovy (semaglutide 2.4 mg) in average weight loss. SURMOUNT-1 showed 20.9% average weight loss at 15 mg tirzepatide versus STEP 1's 14.9% at semaglutide 2.4 mg — though these are separate trials, not head-to-head. The closest real-world comparison, a 2024 JAMA Internal Medicine study, found tirzepatide users were 63% more likely to achieve 5%+ weight loss versus semaglutide users in the first year. Both medications are highly effective; individual access, coverage, tolerability, and cost are often more decisive factors than the average trial results.

Does insurance cover Zepbound?

Coverage is plan-dependent. Most commercial plans with obesity drug benefits cover Zepbound for patients meeting FDA BMI criteria, with prior authorization. Medicare Part D does not cover weight-loss drugs under current law, though the sleep apnea indication may create alternative coverage pathways for some Medicare patients with OSA. State Medicaid coverage is expanding but variable. For a detailed breakdown of coverage requirements and prior authorization tips, see insuranceglp1.com/zepbound-coverage.html.

What happens if you stop taking Zepbound?

SURMOUNT-4 — a dedicated maintenance trial — showed that participants who stopped tirzepatide after initial weight loss regained approximately 14 percentage points of body weight over the following year, while those who continued tirzepatide maintained their loss and achieved additional reduction. A 2026 University of Cambridge modeling study projected weight regain plateauing at about 75% of original loss across GLP-1 class drugs, with roughly 25% of loss maintained long-term. A structured maintenance plan — protein-forward diet, resistance training, and scheduled provider follow-up — is essential before stopping Zepbound.
